Spicewood TX Real Estate 2026: Hill Country Acreage, Krause Springs, and Rural Lake Travis Living

For buyers who want the real Hill Country, spring-fed swimming holes, cedar and oak on acreage, dark skies, and the northwest end of Lake Travis, Spicewood delivers natural beauty and genuine rural character at prices well below comparable Austin-area lakeside communities.

By Shivraj Grewal · Grewal RE Group · Spicewood, TX · ~8 min read

Spicewood sits in a part of the Austin area that most buyers overlook until they discover it, and then wonder why they didn't look sooner. Straddling Travis and Burnet Counties along RR 2322, Hwy 71, and RR 2147, this unincorporated rural community northwest of Austin offers what very few places in the metro can: genuine acreage, Hill Country topography, direct proximity to Lake Travis, a privately operated natural swimming hole that belongs on any Texas bucket list, and land prices that still represent meaningful value compared to the closer-in suburbs. In 2026, Spicewood is drawing serious attention from a new wave of buyers who want land, privacy, and natural beauty without sacrificing reasonable access to the city.

Spicewood Overview: Rural Character and Hill Country Identity

Spicewood (ZIP 78669) is not a city, it is an unincorporated community, which matters for buyers in concrete ways. There is no city government, no city taxes, and no city services in the conventional sense. Water typically comes from a private well or a rural water co-op. Wastewater is handled by a private septic system. Emergency services are provided by volunteer fire departments and the county sheriff. The absence of a municipal layer is a feature, not a bug, for the buyers Spicewood attracts, those who want land on their own terms, minimal regulation, and the ability to keep horses, goats, or agricultural use without navigating city zoning boards.

The topography is quintessential Texas Hill Country. Cedar and live oak cover the hillsides, limestone outcroppings define creek beds and road cuts, and the land undulates in ways that create dramatic building sites with Hill Country views. Travis Peak rises to the east, providing a visual anchor that orients the community. The Pedernales River watershed shapes the ecology of the area, feeding the spring systems that make places like Krause Springs possible.

Access corridors into Spicewood include RR 2322 from Marble Falls to the west and from the Hwy 71 / Bee Cave corridor to the east, Hwy 71 itself running through the southern edge of the community, and RR 2147 providing a more northerly approach. Spicewood Springs Road serves interior portions of the community. These are rural two-lane roads for the most part, beautiful to drive, but not the high-speed arterials that characterize more suburban Austin zip codes. That road character is by design. Spicewood is a place you choose to live in because you want the rural experience, not despite it.

Spicewood Real Estate Market in 2026: Acreage Pricing and Value Compared to Lakeway and Bee Cave

Spicewood's price range in 2026 spans approximately $350,000 to $1.5 million, with the spread driven far more by acreage size and Lake Travis proximity than by home finishes or neighborhood prestige[1]. This is a land-first market. A 3-acre parcel with a modest home might trade at $400K; the same 3 acres with a custom-built home and water views can approach $1M or above. And for raw land, buyers looking to build their own homestead from scratch, Spicewood still offers tracts in the 5–20 acre range at per-acre prices that represent some of the best value in the Austin metro.

The contrast with Lakeway and Bee Cave is stark and instructive. In Bee Cave, the median lot is roughly a quarter to a third of an acre inside a master-planned community with HOA oversight, builder standards, and a suburban infrastructure that constrains what you can do with your property. In Spicewood, buyers routinely purchase 5, 10, or 20-acre tracts that would be unimaginable in the 78738 ZIP code, at prices that are often lower in absolute terms. Lakeway similarly operates in a primarily suburban lot-size paradigm, with water access lots commanding premiums that push median pricing well above what Spicewood acreage demands.

The trade-off is real: Spicewood buyers give up the suburban convenience ecosystem entirely. There is no grocery store in Spicewood proper. The nearest full-service commercial corridor is Marble Falls to the west (approximately 20–25 minutes on Hwy 71) or the Bee Cave / Lakeway area to the east (approximately 25–35 minutes). That commute to amenities is priced into the land. For buyers whose lifestyle genuinely centers around the land itself, the horses, the garden, the views, the creek, the trade-off is more than acceptable.

Days on market for Spicewood properties runs longer than suburban Austin norms, often 60–120 days, reflecting the smaller buyer pool for rural acreage[1]. This actually benefits prepared buyers: well-priced properties are available long enough to allow proper due diligence, and sellers who have been sitting on the market tend to be negotiable in ways that suburban sellers in multiple-offer situations are not.

Krause Springs: Natural Swimming, Camping, and the Heart of Spicewood's Identity

No honest account of Spicewood can be written without leading with Krause Springs. This privately operated natural swimming destination on RR 2322 is one of the most special places in Texas, not a claim made lightly in a state that has Hamilton Pool, Barton Springs, and Jacob's Well competing for that superlative. Krause Springs features a series of spring-fed pools and cascading water flowing through ancient cypress trees, limestone bedrock, and a landscape that feels almost impossibly lush given how much of Hill Country Texas looks in August. The main swimming hole drops over a limestone shelf into a deep, clear pool that maintains a consistent temperature year-round courtesy of the spring system feeding it.

The property is family-owned and has been operated as a swimming and camping destination for generations. Day entry is charged by the person, and the site also offers tent camping and RV hookups under the trees along the creek. Weekends in summer fill early, the property limits capacity and gates can close to new entrants by mid-morning on peak days. For residents of the Spicewood area, Krause Springs functions as the neighborhood pool, the summer social hub, and the primary reason many buyers first fall in love with the community. It is three miles from the nearest subdivision, not a waterpark, and that is precisely the point.

The existence of Krause Springs as a permanent, privately preserved natural landmark is one of Spicewood's genuine assets. It is not publicly owned, so its long-term character depends on the family's continued stewardship, a factor worth acknowledging. But it has operated continuously for decades and shows no signs of changing its character. As a quality-of-life anchor for the community, it is irreplaceable.

Lake Travis Access on the Northwest End

Spicewood sits at the northwest end of Lake Travis, where the lake narrows as it approaches the upper Pedernales arm. This geography means that Lake Travis access in the Spicewood area is different from the experience at more established lake communities like Lago Vista, Lakeway, or Hudson Bend. The lake here is deeper into Hill Country, more remote, less developed, quieter, and in many respects more beautiful for the absence of the marina infrastructure and waterfront dining that characterizes the lake's more urbanized southern shore.

The Lower Colorado River Authority (LCRA) manages the Highland Lakes system, including Lake Travis[4]. Lake Travis water levels fluctuate seasonally and in response to upstream precipitation and LCRA management decisions, a reality that all Lake Travis property buyers must understand regardless of where along the lake they purchase. At the Spicewood end, where the lake is narrower and shallower, low water periods have a proportionally larger impact on usability than they do at the wider southern sections. Buyers considering waterfront or water-access properties in the 78669 area should research historical water level data through LCRA before committing.

That said, the northwest end of Lake Travis offers something the more developed sections do not: genuine seclusion. Boat traffic is lighter. Views are wilder. And for buyers who want lake access as a personal retreat rather than as a social amenity, the upper lake is consistently the preferred setting. Properties with direct lake frontage in this area command significant premiums over comparable inland acreage, and they trade infrequently, making them worth tracking actively with an agent who watches this market.

Schools in Spicewood TX: The Dual-Zone Issue Every Buyer Must Understand

The school district question in Spicewood is one of the most important buyer cautions in this entire guide, and it is not widely understood by buyers approaching the area for the first time. Spicewood straddles the boundary between two independent school districts: Marble Falls ISD to the west and Lake Travis ISD to the east[2][3]. Which district serves a specific property depends on the exact street address, and the boundary does not follow the county line cleanly.

This is not a theoretical concern. Two properties on the same road, separated by a few tenths of a mile, can be in different school districts. The difference matters because Marble Falls ISD and Lake Travis ISD serve different communities with different campus configurations, extracurricular cultures, and college preparation pathways. Neither is a poor choice, both are respected districts with dedicated educators and reasonable academic outcomes, but they are meaningfully different, and families with specific school priorities need to verify their assignment before submitting an offer, not after.

The verification process is straightforward: use the address lookup tools on the Marble Falls ISD website or the Lake Travis ISD website to confirm the assigned district for any specific street address. Alternatively, call the district offices directly with the address. Do not rely on listing descriptions, neighborhood rumors, or assumed boundaries based on county lines. This is one of the few areas in the home-buying process where I will tell every Spicewood buyer emphatically: verify it yourself from the district, in writing, before you are under contract.

Rural Lifestyle Considerations: Well Water, Septic, Ag Exemptions, and Dark Skies

Buying acreage in Spicewood is a fundamentally different transaction than buying a home in a master-planned Austin suburb, and the due diligence list reflects that. Here are the key rural-specific considerations every buyer must address.

Water supply. Most Spicewood properties rely on a private well for potable water. Well performance, depth, yield, and water quality, varies significantly across the Hill Country limestone aquifer system. Any acreage purchase should include a professional water well inspection and water quality test as standard due diligence items, not optional add-ons. Well yield matters particularly for properties where irrigation, livestock watering, or multiple structures are contemplated. Some areas of 78669 are served by a rural water supply corporation (WSC), which provides treated municipal-quality water via underground distribution lines, confirm whether a property is on well or co-op water before assuming either.

Wastewater. Septic systems handle wastewater on essentially all Spicewood properties. The type, size, and condition of the existing system should be evaluated by a licensed septic inspector. Conventional systems, aerobic treatment units (ATUs), and low-pressure dosing systems all appear in this area. If you are planning to add square footage, add occupants, or subdivide in the future, the septic system's permitted capacity and the available drain field area become planning constraints that must be understood upfront.

Agricultural exemptions. Many Spicewood properties carry an agricultural (ag) exemption under the Texas Tax Code, which dramatically reduces the assessed value for property tax purposes[1]. Qualifying uses include livestock grazing, hay production, wildlife management, and beekeeping, among others. Ag exemptions do not transfer automatically with sale, the new owner must establish their own qualifying agricultural use within a specific window or risk a rollback tax assessment covering up to five years of the tax savings. Understanding whether a property has an active ag exemption, what use supports it, and what continuation or re-application requirements apply is essential financial due diligence on any Spicewood purchase.

Dark skies. This one is a quality-of-life item rather than a legal consideration, but it belongs on any honest list of Spicewood's attributes. The community is genuinely dark at night in ways that central Austin and even outer suburbs like Pflugerville or Cedar Park are not. The Milky Way is visible on clear nights. The absence of light pollution is a direct consequence of Spicewood's low density and rural character, an amenity that cannot be manufactured and that disappears as development intensifies. For buyers who value the night sky, Spicewood's current character is worth appreciating and worth protecting through mindful site lighting decisions on any property they develop.

Buying Tips for Spicewood Acreage: Due Diligence, Utility Access, and Deed Restrictions

Acreage purchases require a longer and more complex due diligence checklist than suburban transactions. The following items are specific to Spicewood and the rural Hill Country market.

Survey and boundaries. An accurate survey is non-negotiable on any acreage purchase. Rural plat maps and historic fence lines do not always correspond to legal property boundaries. Commission a new survey by a licensed Texas land surveyor if the existing survey is more than a few years old or if any boundary lines are unclear.

Deed restrictions vs. unrestricted. Spicewood is a mixed landscape of deed-restricted properties (particularly in older rural subdivisions that established covenants years ago) and completely unrestricted tracts. Unrestricted land offers maximum flexibility, you can build what you want, keep what animals you choose, and operate most rural businesses without seeking approval from a homeowners' association. But it also means your neighbor has the same freedom, which can be a feature or a concern depending on what adjacently permitted uses you might encounter. Confirm whether a property has any recorded deed restrictions, and review them fully if they exist.

Utility access and costs. Extending electric service to a rural property can range from modest to very expensive depending on distance to the nearest PEDERNALES ELECTRIC COOPERATIVE (PEC) line. Confirm service availability and the cost of any required line extension before finalizing your budget. Internet access varies considerably across 78669, fiber is expanding in parts of the area, but satellite internet (Starlink performs well in rural Hill Country) may be the practical reality on some tracts. Propane tanks handle gas where natural gas lines do not reach.

Flood zone awareness. Hill Country terrain creates dramatic drainage patterns. Properties near creeks, draws, or low-lying areas should be evaluated for FEMA flood zone status and reviewed with a floodplain analysis. The Edwards Plateau is subject to severe flash flooding events, and the topography that makes Spicewood beautiful also makes certain low-lying areas genuinely flood-prone. Review the FEMA flood map for any property near drainageways before proceeding.

Spicewood vs. Lago Vista vs. Marble Falls: The Northwest Corridor Comparison

Buyers exploring the northwest Austin corridor typically compare Spicewood against Lago Vista and Marble Falls, three distinct communities that each serve a different buyer profile along the same geographic band.

Spicewood (78669) is the most rural of the three. It offers the largest average lot sizes, the least commercial infrastructure, the strongest off-grid and ag-exempt character, and prices that reflect both its rural remoteness and its proximity to the northwest end of Lake Travis. Krause Springs is Spicewood's crown jewel. Buyers here are trading suburban convenience for land, privacy, and natural immersion. The school district dual-zone issue requires careful navigation. This is the choice for buyers whose priority hierarchy starts with acreage and ends with HOAs.

Lago Vista sits on the north shore of Lake Travis in Travis County, offering a more organized residential character than Spicewood while retaining a small-town feel. Lago Vista has its own city government, city utilities in most areas, and a more conventional single-family home market. The community's lake access, through multiple water access association parks with boat ramps, fishing piers, and swimming areas, is its primary draw[4]. Lago Vista ISD serves the community with a tight-knit campus culture. Prices overlap with Spicewood's lower range but trend higher for lakefront and lake-access properties. Buyers who want a recognized small town on the lake with city water and services tend to prefer Lago Vista over the more unstructured Spicewood.

Marble Falls is a proper city with a growing downtown, city utilities, direct Lake LBJ and Lake Marble Falls access, and a commercial amenity base that Spicewood entirely lacks. It serves buyers who want Hill Country character plus a walkable small-city downtown, restaurants, and a real grocery store within minutes of home. Marble Falls ISD is the school district here. Prices range from surprisingly affordable in established neighborhoods to significant for lakefront estates. For buyers who want Hill Country life but feel the pull of a real town center, Marble Falls often wins the comparison. It is roughly 30–35 minutes from Spicewood's core via Hwy 71, making it accessible for errands even for Spicewood residents.

The northwest corridor as a whole represents one of the most compelling value propositions in the Austin real estate market in 2026. As central Austin and near-west suburbs have repriced beyond the reach of many buyers, the communities along Hwy 71 and RR 2322 offer an authentic Texas lifestyle at prices that still make mathematical sense, particularly for buyers willing to embrace the rural character that comes with the territory.
