# Liberty Hill TX Real Estate 2026: Santa Rita Ranch, Top Schools, and Hill Country Growth

Liberty Hill has quietly become one of the most compelling growth stories in northwest Williamson County, award-winning master-planned communities, a school district expanding alongside the city it serves, genuine Hill Country character, and home prices that still deliver more space per dollar than its closer-in neighbors. Here is what the 2026 market looks like and what buyers and sellers need to know.

By **Shivraj Grewal** · Grewal RE Group · Liberty Hill, TX · ~8 min read

Liberty Hill sits at a particular moment in the growth arc of northwest Austin that does not come twice. It is far enough from the city core that the Hill Country landscape is real, rolling terrain, cedar and live oak, the Gabriel River threading through the countryside, and close enough that Leander's MetroRail station is a short drive away and Austin's employment centers remain accessible by highway. The city's master-planned communities have drawn national recognition. Its school district is expanding to meet demand rather than straining under it. And for buyers who have looked at Cedar Park and Leander and found them either too expensive or too built-out, Liberty Hill offers a version of the same northwest Williamson County lifestyle at a meaningful pricing discount. Understanding why that is the case, and what the trade-offs actually are, is what this guide is about.

## Liberty Hill Overview: Hill Country Growth, More Space, and Award-Winning Communities

Liberty Hill occupies the northwest edge of Williamson County, centered on the intersection of US-183 and Hwy 29, with Inner Loop Road and CR 200 forming the backbone of the city's internal grid as development has expanded in every direction. The city is in Williamson County, the same county as Round Rock, Georgetown, and Cedar Park, but its position at the far northwest corner gives it a character that reads more like Hill Country than suburban corridor. The terrain here is noticeably different from what you find in Pflugerville or Hutto: hillier, more dramatic, with the cedar-studded ridge lines and limestone outcroppings that define the eastern edge of the Edwards Plateau.

The growth story is straightforward. Liberty Hill's population has expanded rapidly over the past decade as buyers have followed the northwest corridor past Leander looking for newer construction, lower price points, and larger lots. The infrastructure has generally kept pace, new roads, school campuses added as enrollment climbs, commercial development accelerating along US-183 to serve the growing residential base. The city is not fully built-out in the way that Cedar Park essentially is; land is still available for new development, and the construction pipeline remains active across multiple master-planned communities and infill tracts. For buyers, that means new-construction options across a wide price spectrum, often from multiple competing builders within the same community, a dynamic that tends to keep pricing competitive and give buyers more negotiating leverage than they find in resale-heavy markets.

The defining amenity achievement here is Santa Rita Ranch, which has placed Liberty Hill on the national master-planned community map in a way that few Texas cities of its size can claim. That community, and what it represents about Liberty Hill's growth trajectory, warrants its own section, which follows below.

## Liberty Hill Real Estate Market in 2026: Prices, Appreciation, and New Construction Activity

The Liberty Hill real estate market in 2026 is primarily a new-construction market, with resale inventory growing as the city matures but new builds still dominating buyer activity in most price segments[[1]](#src-1). That distinction matters because it shapes negotiating dynamics, timeline expectations, and the due diligence process in ways that differ from buying a resale home in an established neighborhood.

The **entry-level new construction** range in Liberty Hill starts around **$380,000 to $450,000** for three-bedroom homes of 1,800 to 2,200 square feet, typically from production builders in communities along US-183 and within established master-planned developments. These homes compete directly with what buyers find in the southern Leander and northeast Cedar Park markets, but with newer construction and, in communities like Santa Rita Ranch, significantly more developed amenity infrastructure. The value proposition is real for buyers whose budget is capped in this range.

The **move-up segment**, running from approximately **$480,000 to $650,000**, is the most actively traded tier in Liberty Hill in 2026. Four-bedroom homes of 2,500 to 3,500 square feet with two-car garages, open-plan living, and full HOA amenity access dominate this segment. Buyers here are typically comparing Liberty Hill to similar homes in Leander's newer communities and finding that the comparable Liberty Hill home prices $50,000 to $80,000 lower for equivalent square footage and build quality, a gap that has persisted through multiple market cycles because Liberty Hill's greater distance from Austin continues to discount it relative to the closer-in market[[1]](#src-1).

At the upper end of the Liberty Hill market, custom builds and premium lots within Santa Rita Ranch and similar communities reach **$700,000 to $750,000 and above**, with larger footprints, upgraded finishes, and in some cases expanded lot sizes that include meaningful rear yard space. Acreage properties outside the master-planned communities, available on county roads radiating from the city center, can exceed these figures depending on land size, improvements, and water supply.

The appreciation trajectory since 2020 mirrors the broader northwest Austin story: a significant run-up through mid-2022 followed by a moderate normalization in 2023, with values stabilizing and in some segments recovering in 2024–2025. Liberty Hill's correction was shallower than what occurred in some more speculative new-construction markets because the underlying demand drivers, school quality, community investment in Santa Rita Ranch, and the price advantage relative to neighboring cities, remained intact throughout the cycle.

## Santa Rita Ranch: What Makes It One of America's Most Award-Winning Communities

Santa Rita Ranch has become the anchor story of Liberty Hill real estate, and for good reason. The community has received national recognition as one of America's best master-planned communities for multiple consecutive years, an achievement that is not common for Texas communities outside the Houston metro's most established names. Understanding why it has earned that recognition, and what it actually delivers day-to-day for residents, is essential context for any buyer evaluating Liberty Hill.

The amenity program at Santa Rita Ranch is genuinely resort-caliber. Multiple pools, including resort-style main pools with water slides, splash pads, and zero-entry designs, anchor each of the community's amenity centers, which also include fitness facilities, event spaces, and social programming. Fishing lakes are stocked and accessible to residents throughout the year, providing a recreational asset that is unusual in a master-planned suburban context and that connects to the Hill Country character of the surrounding landscape. The trail network runs through the community's green space and connects to parks, playgrounds, and open areas that give the development a landscape feel notably less dense than many comparable Austin-area master-planned communities.

What distinguishes Santa Rita Ranch from communities that have amenities without identity is the deliberate community culture that the developer has built around those amenities. Resident events, outdoor movie nights, holiday gatherings, seasonal programming at the amenity centers, organized fishing tournaments on the lakes, are regular calendar features rather than occasional marketing gestures. Residents frequently cite this programming as a reason the community feels cohesive rather than anonymous, which is the failure mode of many large master-planned developments that achieve physical amenity density but never develop social texture.

The housing product within Santa Rita Ranch spans multiple price points and builder offerings, with production and semi-custom builders each represented. This variety means buyers can access the community's amenity platform at different price tiers, from entry-level townhomes to larger custom-adjacent single-family homes, without being locked into a single builder's product or pricing structure. For families relocating from markets where master-planned communities often mean a single builder monopoly and limited customization options, this represents a more competitive and buyer-friendly structure than they may be accustomed to[[4]](#src-4). Primary builders active across Santa Rita Ranch and the broader Liberty Hill market in 2026 include Brightland Homes, Perry Homes, Pacesetter Homes, David Weekley Homes, and Taylor Morrison, along with regional custom builders serving the acreage and luxury segments.

Santa Rita Ranch is the anchor, but it is not the only planned community shaping the Liberty Hill market. Several other developments give buyers additional entry points and lot characters: **Rancho Santa Clara** (roughly $380K–$620K, new construction with a community pool), **Northgate Ranch** (roughly $360K–$580K, notable for larger 8,000–20,000 sq ft lots and a more rural character), **Orchard Ridge** (roughly $380K–$600K, in active buildout with its own amenities), and value-oriented sections like Saddleback and Live Oak in the $350K–$550K range. Lot sizes across these communities generally run from about 6,000 square feet up to a quarter acre, giving buyers more usable yard than comparable Leander or Cedar Park subdivisions at similar price points. ## Liberty Hill ISD: A Growing District Built for a Growing City

Liberty Hill ISD is the school district that serves this market, and it has been one of the defining growth stories in Williamson County education over the past decade[[2]](#src-2). The district has added campuses at a pace that mirrors the city's population growth, a notable contrast with some fast-growing Texas communities whose school infrastructure has lagged their residential expansion, creating overcrowding and boundary volatility that undermines the school quality story buyers came for in the first place.

**Liberty Hill High School** is the flagship campus and serves as the heart of the community's athletic and academic identity. The high school hosts competitive varsity programs across football, basketball, baseball, softball, soccer, and other sports, with a community culture around athletics that is characteristic of the Hill Country belt of Central Texas. Academic programming includes advanced coursework, dual-enrollment options, and the full range of extracurricular offerings that families expect from a well-resourced modern high school. The district's continued enrollment growth has driven investment in facilities that remain relatively new compared to more established suburban districts in the metro.

The district's rapid growth has also produced a second comprehensive high school: **Santa Rita High School**, a newly opened campus serving the fast-growing southwest sections of the district, including Santa Rita Ranch residents, with modern facilities and a strong early trajectory. Middle-grade students across much of the community are served by Stiles Middle School. At the elementary and middle school level, Liberty Hill ISD has expanded its campus inventory to accommodate the waves of new families arriving as Santa Rita Ranch and the city's other master-planned communities come online. New campuses mean newer buildings with modern classroom infrastructure, an advantage over older districts where students attend aging facilities that reflect decades-old construction rather than current educational design standards. Buyers should always verify current school zone assignments for specific addresses using the Liberty Hill ISD online address lookup, as enrollment boundaries have continued to shift in step with the district's expansion[[2]](#src-2).

The district's relationship with the city government and development community has generally been collaborative rather than adversarial, coordination on school siting within new master-planned communities has allowed Liberty Hill ISD to open campuses that are genuinely integrated into the neighborhoods they serve rather than located on outlying parcels that require long bus routes. That planning coherence is a quality-of-life detail that matters to families with young children and that reflects well on the long-term planning orientation of both the city and the district.

## Hill Country Character: Gabriel River, Natural Beauty, and Acreage Options

One of Liberty Hill's genuine differentiators in the northwest Williamson County market is that the Hill Country here is not just a marketing phrase, it is the actual landscape. The terrain is noticeably more dramatic than what buyers find in the flat-to-gently-rolling ground of Round Rock, Pflugerville, or the eastern portions of Cedar Park. Limestone outcroppings, cedar-studded ridgelines, and seasonal drainage systems that define the eastern Edwards Plateau are a routine feature of the Liberty Hill landscape, and the Gabriel River, which flows through Williamson County south and east of the city, provides the kind of natural water feature that anchors outdoor recreation in a region where live water is genuinely prized.

For buyers interested in acreage rather than master-planned subdivision living, Liberty Hill and the surrounding Williamson County countryside offer options at price points that are dramatically lower than comparable acreage in Dripping Springs or Driftwood to the south. Properties on CR 200, the county roads branching off Hwy 29, and the rural corridors between Liberty Hill and the Burnet County line offer one-to-ten-acre parcels with Hill Country character, older native tree cover, and the kind of privacy and land utility, livestock keeping, outbuildings, home-based businesses, that deed-restricted HOA communities cannot accommodate. Water supply on rural Williamson County properties typically draws from private wells; well depth and yield vary, and a thorough inspection by a licensed professional before purchase is non-negotiable on any acreage transaction.

The acreage market segments into a few recognizable types. Turn-key horse properties with existing infrastructure, barn, fencing, round pen, pasture, and water access, trade actively in roughly the $600K–$1.2M range. Raw, unimproved land for a custom build ranges widely by size and utilities, and mini-ranch lifestyle properties of three to ten acres with a home typically run $700K to $1.5M. The single most important financial variable on many of these parcels is the agricultural exemption: qualifying properties that carry and maintain an ag exemption can see effective property-tax liability reduced by roughly 50–80%, which materially changes the carrying cost of a large tract[[4]](#src-4). Buyers should verify the exemption status and its continuation requirements, along with well and septic condition, deed restrictions, and Williamson County livestock allowances, as core due-diligence items before closing on any acreage purchase.

The contrast between the master-planned community experience in Santa Rita Ranch and the rural acreage experience available a few miles away is one of the things that makes Liberty Hill a more varied market than its size might suggest. The same ZIP code contains both, and buyers who take the time to understand both options are better positioned to make a choice that matches their actual lifestyle needs rather than defaulting to the most-marketed product.

## Proximity and Commute: MetroRail, US-183, and an Honest Assessment

Commute reality is where Liberty Hill's pricing advantage requires honest calibration. The city sits approximately 35–40 miles northwest of downtown Austin, and the drive via US-183 through Leander and Cedar Park runs approximately **45 to 60 minutes under normal conditions**, with peak-hour windows on US-183 and the subsequent connector to MoPac or I-35 extending that to 70–90 minutes on busy mornings[[3]](#src-3). This is a meaningful commute for anyone making the drive five days a week, and buyers should factor it into their decision with eyes open rather than relying on off-peak test drives to assess their actual daily experience.

The MetroRail option provides real mitigation for buyers whose employers are reachable from the Capitol MetroRail line. **Leander Station**, the line's northwestern terminus, is located approximately 15 to 20 minutes from most Liberty Hill addresses by car. From Leander Station, the Capital MetroRail Red Line connects south to downtown Austin (Republic Square Station and the Seaholm District) in approximately 60 minutes, stopping at Howard, McNeil, Domain/Kramer, Crestview, and MLK along the way. For buyers working in the Domain, Mueller, or downtown Austin corridors who are comfortable with a park-and-ride model, this commute profile is genuinely manageable, particularly as hybrid work schedules have normalized one-to-three in-office days per week as the standard for much of Austin's professional workforce. For buyers who need to be in an Austin office daily and whose employer is not near the rail line, the commute calculus is harder and should be resolved before committing to a Liberty Hill purchase.

US-183 corridor development, including the ongoing 183A Turnpike extension projects, has been a long-running factor in reducing travel times within the northwest corridor, and continued infrastructure investment in this section of Williamson County is an established political priority. The picture in five years will likely be incrementally better than it is today, but buyers making a near-term decision should plan around current conditions rather than projected improvements.

## Retail and Services: The Honest Trade-Off

Liberty Hill's retail and services landscape is still catching up to its population, and this is one of the market's few genuine trade-offs against closer-in Austin suburbs. For now, residents rely primarily on Cedar Park and Georgetown for major grocery, dining, and services: HEB Plus, Costco, Target, and a deeper restaurant roster sit roughly 20–30 minutes south in Cedar Park, and Georgetown's square and I-35 retail are 20–25 minutes east. A new HEB has been announced and is in development for Liberty Hill itself, which will be a meaningful quality-of-life upgrade for residents currently driving to neighboring cities for full-service grocery access, and both the Hwy 29 corridor and Ronald Reagan Boulevard are expected to see significant commercial buildout over the next several years. Buyers accustomed to neighborhood-walkable retail should set expectations accordingly; the same distance that discounts Liberty Hill's home prices is also why its retail base lags, and that gap is closing rather than widening.

## Buying Tips: Master-Planned vs. Acreage, Commute Reality, and Utility Districts

Buying in Liberty Hill involves a set of decision points that are somewhat specific to a city at this particular stage of growth, not yet fully built-out, with multiple builder relationships active simultaneously, and with a meaningful gap between the master-planned community experience and the rural acreage experience available nearby. Working through these clearly before making an offer will save time and reduce surprises during the transaction.

**Master-planned vs. acreage** is the first branching decision. Santa Rita Ranch and the city's other HOA communities offer resort amenities, newer construction, and the community infrastructure of a well-managed planned development, but they come with deed restrictions, HOA dues (typically $800–$1,200 annually in the Liberty Hill market), and architectural review requirements. Acreage properties outside these communities typically have no HOA, unrestricted or lightly deed-restricted land use, and the freedom to build outbuildings, keep animals, or subdivide in the future, but they require more active stewardship of infrastructure (well, septic, road maintenance) and offer less of the instant amenity gratification that master-planned communities provide. Neither is categorically better; they serve different buyers with different priorities.

**Utility districts** are an important due diligence item in a fast-growing area like Liberty Hill. Many properties in and around Liberty Hill are served by Municipal Utility Districts (MUDs) rather than city utilities directly, MUD assessments are reflected in the effective property tax rate and can be meaningful, sometimes adding 0.5% to 1.0% to the total effective rate above the base city and county levy. In practice, the combined rate (Williamson County + Liberty Hill ISD + city + any applicable MUD) typically lands in the range of roughly 2.1% to 2.8% of assessed value depending on the specific subdivision and district[[4]](#src-4). Understanding which utility district serves a specific property, what the current tax rate is, and when any bond-funded infrastructure is expected to be paid off is important financial context that belongs in the early due diligence phase of any Liberty Hill purchase, not as an afterthought at the closing table.

**New-construction timing** is another Liberty Hill-specific consideration. Many buyers in this market are purchasing from builders with six-to-twelve-month construction timelines, meaning the due diligence cycle includes not just property-level inspection but also builder vetting, contract review, and systematic third-party inspections at foundation, frame, and pre-drywall stages. Builder contracts in this market are generally builder-friendly, negotiate what you can on upgrades and closing cost contributions, understand the earnest money and delay provisions thoroughly, and consider a buyer's agent who has active builder relationships in this specific market.

## Liberty Hill vs. Leander vs. Georgetown: NW Williamson County Compared

Buyers evaluating northwest Williamson County frequently compare Liberty Hill, Leander, and Georgetown on the same shortlist. All three sit within roughly 20 miles of one another along the US-183 and I-35 corridors, all three are in Williamson County, and all three offer master-planned community options, newer construction, and strong school districts. The distinctions between them matter and map clearly to different buyer profiles.

**Leander** is the most supply-constrained of the three at this point in its growth cycle, a city that was one of the fastest-growing in the United States for several consecutive years and is now substantially built-out in its most desirable areas. Leander ISD is one of the most highly regarded school districts in Texas, with Leander High School, Vandegrift High School, and Cedar Park High School as flagship campuses. The proximity to the MetroRail station at Leander makes it the strongest commuter option of the three, shorter driving distance to the station, more walkable in some neighborhoods than Liberty Hill. But that desirability is priced in: comparable homes in Leander cost noticeably more than in Liberty Hill, and new construction options are increasingly limited to infill and final-phase development rather than the large-scale community building that characterized Leander's earlier growth. Buyers who want the most established northwest Austin suburban experience and are willing to pay for it will typically find Leander the right answer. Buyers who want more house, more land, and more upside from continued growth at a lower entry point will often find Liberty Hill more compelling[[1]](#src-1).

**Georgetown** sits to the east of Liberty Hill along I-35, about 15 miles from the city center. Georgetown ISD is well-regarded, the city's historic downtown square is a genuine asset that most northwest corridor suburbs lack, and Georgetown has attracted significant new-construction investment in master-planned communities including Wolf Ranch. Pricing is broadly similar to Liberty Hill, sometimes slightly higher in Georgetown's most desirable near-town neighborhoods, sometimes lower in the city's eastern and southeastern outskirts. Georgetown's commute to Austin is comparable: downtown Austin is roughly 35–45 minutes via I-35 under normal conditions. The distinction between Georgetown and Liberty Hill is primarily one of character and lifestyle orientation: Georgetown offers the walkable historic downtown, proximity to Southwestern University, and the established-town feel that comes from a city with genuine century-old bones. Liberty Hill offers the Hill Country setting, Santa Rita Ranch's amenity platform, and a sense of frontier growth energy that comes from being in an earlier phase of development. Buyers who value historic town character will tend toward Georgetown; those who value Hill Country landscape and newer community infrastructure will tend toward Liberty Hill.

The comparison is ultimately a conversation about which combination of price point, commute tolerance, school district preference, community character, and land use priorities fits your specific situation.
