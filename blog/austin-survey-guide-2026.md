When you buy a home in Texas, the purchase contract, the inspection, and the title commitment all get attention from the first day of the option period. The survey often gets attention last, or not at all, until something goes wrong at closing. That is a mistake. A Texas land survey does more than locate your property lines on a map. It reveals encroachments, easements, setback violations, and flood zone boundaries that can affect what you can build, what your title insurance covers, and what your property is actually worth. Every Austin buyer should understand surveys before they write an offer, not after the option period has expired.

## Why Surveys Matter in Austin Real Estate

A land survey physically locates and marks your property boundaries using recorded legal descriptions and reference monuments set by the county. In Austin's dense urban core and sprawling Hill Country fringe alike, those boundaries are rarely as obvious as a fence line suggests. Surveys reveal information that no inspection, no title search, and no walkthrough can uncover on its own.

The most consequential things a survey can reveal include encroachments, instances where a neighbor's fence, garage, driveway, or outbuilding physically sits on your property. They also reveal easements: utility corridors, drainage paths, and access rights that allow others to use portions of your land for specific purposes. Setback violations, where an existing structure sits closer to a property line than local zoning allows, show up on surveys and can create financing problems or prevent future expansion. Flood zone delineations drawn across a property can affect which flood insurance zone applies and at what premium. And surveys confirm whether the lot dimensions match what the seller represented in the listing.

Texas title companies require a survey or a survey exception before issuing an owner's title policy. Without a current, approved survey, your title policy will contain what is called a Schedule B-2 survey exception, meaning the policy explicitly does not insure against boundary disputes, encroachments, or anything that a current survey would have revealed. For most urban Austin lots, buyers accept this exception. For rural tracts, Hill Country acreage, or properties with complicated boundary histories, accepting the survey exception is a significant risk.

## Survey types, typical Austin cost ranges, and best use cases, based on 2026 licensed surveyor pricing data. Cost varies by lot size, age, and boundary complexity. Who Orders the Survey in Austin?

In Texas, the responsibility for ordering a survey is entirely negotiable between buyer and seller, and the Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) contract promulgates the question directly: will the seller furnish an existing survey, or will the buyer obtain a new one? This negotiation point is written into the One to Four Family Residential Contract at paragraph 6.C.

Traditionally, sellers provide an existing survey if they have one. Buyers should examine that existing survey carefully before accepting it. An existing survey should be viewed with skepticism if it is more than five years old, if any improvements have been added to the property since it was completed (a new fence, pool, deck, shed, or addition), if the seller cannot provide the surveyor's certification page, or if boundary conditions appear unusual. In any of these cases, ordering a new survey is strongly advisable.

The cost of a new boundary survey for a standard Austin residential lot typically runs $600 to $1,200, depending on lot size and complexity. In Hill Country or rural Travis County, where metes-and-bounds descriptions are common and lots are larger, costs can run higher. The buyer typically pays for a new survey, because the primary beneficiary of the survey, and the resulting removal of the survey exception from the title policy, is the buyer. In a competitive market, buyers often absorb this cost without negotiation. In a balanced or buyer-favorable market, it is worth requesting the seller contribute to survey costs or provide an existing survey of acceptable vintage.

## Reading Your Survey, Key Elements to Understand

A Texas boundary survey is a legal document prepared by a licensed professional surveyor and drawn to scale. Reading one is a skill, but every buyer should understand its major components before signing off on the option period.

**Monument markers** are the physical iron pins, concrete monuments, or rebar stakes that a surveyor sets or confirms at the corners and along the lines of your property. These are the ground-truth of the survey. **Bearings and distances** form the legal description: each side of your property boundary is described by a compass bearing (e.g., N 45° 30' E) and a distance in feet, which together precisely define the shape and location of your lot.

**Easements** are among the most important items a survey reveals. Utility easements allow electric, water, gas, and telecommunications companies to run infrastructure across your property and maintain it, often meaning you cannot build a permanent structure over that corridor. Drainage easements allow water flow across private land during storm events. Access easements give adjoining property owners or the public the right to cross your land. All recorded easements should appear on the survey, and buyers should understand what each one allows and prohibits.

**Setback lines** show the minimum distance from each property line within which construction is prohibited by local zoning or deed restrictions. If an existing structure sits within a setback, that can affect financing and future renovation permits. **Flood zone delineations** drawn from FEMA Flood Insurance Rate Maps (FIRMs) may cross your property, placing portions in different flood zones with different insurance implications. **Impervious cover calculations** show the current percentage of your lot covered by hard surfaces, a critical number if you plan to add a pool, expanded driveway, or additional structure.

## Survey Exceptions in Your Title Commitment

When your title company issues a title commitment, the preliminary document that outlines what your title insurance policy will and will not cover, it includes a Schedule B-2 section listing exceptions from coverage. One of the most common is the survey exception, which reads roughly: "any encroachments, overlaps, boundary line disputes, and any other matters which would be disclosed by an accurate survey and inspection of the premises."

In plain terms: your title insurance policy will not protect you against boundary problems unless you remove this exception by providing a survey the title company approves. Removing the survey exception requires providing either a new boundary survey or an existing survey that meets the title company's standards, typically current enough and comprehensive enough that the title company is willing to underwrite against survey-related claims.

For properties in Austin's urban core with straightforward rectangular lots and recent surveys, many buyers accept the survey exception as a reasonable calculated risk. The risk is low when the property has been in the same configuration for decades, neighbors are cooperative, and boundary lines are obvious. The calculus changes dramatically for Hill Country tracts, properties with creek frontage (where boundaries often follow the water's edge), older properties with metes-and-bounds legal descriptions, corner lots, or any property where the listing description mentions any boundary uncertainty. For these properties, removing the survey exception is not optional, it is essential.

## Austin-Specific Survey Issues

Austin has several regulatory layers that make surveys particularly consequential compared to many other Texas markets. The most significant is **impervious cover regulation**. The City of Austin imposes strict limits on the percentage of a lot that can be covered by impervious surfaces, concrete, asphalt, roofs, and similar hard materials that prevent water from absorbing into the ground. These limits range from as high as 45% in some suburban zoning districts to as low as 15% in the Barton Springs Zone, which covers a large swath of southwest Austin near the Barton Creek watershed.

When a buyer wants to add a pool, expand the footprint of the house, add a garage, or install a sport court, the impervious cover calculation on the existing survey determines what is possible. A survey that shows the property is already at 38% impervious cover in a 45%-maximum district leaves limited room for additions. Buyers with expansion plans should always verify impervious cover headroom against their intended improvements before closing.

**Metes-and-bounds descriptions** are common in older Austin neighborhoods, particularly central Austin properties platted before the city adopted a standard grid, and throughout the Hill Country and rural Travis County. These descriptions use compass bearings, distances, and references to historical monuments to define property boundaries without a conventional plat. They can be difficult to interpret without a licensed surveyor and are more prone to ambiguity than standard platted lot descriptions. Any property with a metes-and-bounds legal description should have a current survey, not just for your protection, but because your title company will likely require it anyway.[[5]](#src-5)

## Flood Elevation Certificates, When You Need One

A Flood Elevation Certificate (EC) is a specific document prepared by a licensed surveyor that establishes the elevation of your structure relative to the Base Flood Elevation (BFE), the elevation that FEMA has determined floodwaters would reach during a 100-year flood event. Flood Elevation Certificates are distinct from a standard boundary survey, though the same surveyor can often provide both.[[2]](#src-2)

Lenders are required by law to obtain flood insurance on any federally backed mortgage for a property located in a Special Flood Hazard Area, specifically FEMA Zone AE (the 100-year floodplain). To determine the appropriate insurance premium, the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) uses the Flood Elevation Certificate to compare the structure's lowest floor elevation against the BFE.

The financial stakes are significant. A property whose lowest floor is one foot above the Base Flood Elevation might carry an NFIP premium of $2,800 to $3,200 per year. A property three feet above BFE on the same flood map might pay $700 to $900 per year, a difference that compounds to tens of thousands of dollars over a 30-year mortgage. If an existing Flood Elevation Certificate is on file with the property, review it before closing. If none exists and the property is in or near a flood zone, order one. The cost ($400–$700) can pay for itself in the first year's insurance savings alone if the structure's elevation is favorable.

Austin's waterways, Barton Creek, Bull Creek, Shoal Creek, Onion Creek, the Colorado River corridor, create extensive flood zone exposure throughout the metro. Travis County and the City of Austin have both adopted more stringent local floodplain management ordinances than FEMA's minimum requirements, meaning that even properties outside the mapped 100-year floodplain may face local restrictions. The City of Austin Watershed Protection Department maintains local floodplain mapping that buyers should consult alongside FEMA's Flood Map Service Center data.[[4]](#src-4)

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I need a new survey when buying a house in Texas?:**

Not always. Texas title companies will accept an existing survey if it is current, meets their standards, and the seller provides an affidavit that no improvements have been made to the property since the survey was completed. However, if the existing survey is more than five years old, if improvements have been added (fences, pools, additions, sheds), or if boundary conditions are unclear, you should order a new survey. A new boundary survey in Austin typically costs $600–$1,200 and allows you to remove the survey exception from your title insurance policy, a meaningful protection for most buyers.

**Q: How much does a land survey cost in Austin TX?:**

Survey costs in Austin depend on type and lot complexity. A standard residential boundary survey runs $600–$1,200. An ALTA/NSPS survey, required for commercial transactions and many institutional lenders, costs $1,500–$4,000. Foundation surveys for new construction are $400–$700. Flood Elevation Certificates run $400–$700. Mortgage location surveys (for lender use only; not insurable) are the most affordable at $350–$600. Lot size, number of corners, existence of older monuments, and boundary complexity all affect final pricing from the surveyor.

**Q: What does a Texas survey reveal?:**

A Texas boundary survey reveals the precise property lines and corner monuments, all recorded easements (utility, drainage, and access), setback lines showing minimum build distances from boundaries, any encroachments where a neighbor's fence or structure crosses your property line, flood zone delineations, impervious cover calculations, and actual lot dimensions compared to the seller's representations. For properties in flood zones, a Flood Elevation Certificate also documents the structure's elevation relative to the Base Flood Elevation, which directly determines flood insurance premiums under the National Flood Insurance Program.

**Q: Who pays for the survey in a Texas real estate transaction?:**

Survey payment is negotiable in Texas. Traditionally, sellers provide an existing survey if available. When a new survey is required, because the existing one is outdated, improvements have been added, or the buyer's lender requires it, the cost is negotiated as part of the contract. Buyers frequently pay for new surveys because they are the primary beneficiaries: the survey enables removal of the survey exception from their title insurance policy. In competitive Austin markets, buyers often absorb the survey cost without negotiation. In buyer-favorable markets, requesting seller contribution is a reasonable ask.
