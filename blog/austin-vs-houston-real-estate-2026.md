## Price and Appreciation: Austin vs Houston Real Estate

The most immediate difference between Austin and Houston real estate is price. As of mid-2026, Austin's median home sale price hovers around $485,000, while Houston's sits near $320,000, a gap of roughly $165,000 or 52%. For many buyers, especially those relocating from expensive coastal markets, Houston can feel like an extraordinary bargain.

But price is only part of the story. According to data from [Texas A&M's Real Estate Center (TRERC)](https://www.recenter.tamu.edu/), Austin-area home values have appreciated approximately 41% over the past five years, while Houston has posted roughly 28% gains over the same period. This gap reflects several structural forces: Austin's constrained geography (the Hill Country creates a natural western wall), a concentrated high-income tech workforce anchored by Apple, Tesla, Meta, and Google, and an ongoing in-migration wave from California, New York, and the Pacific Northwest.

Houston's more modest appreciation is not a sign of weakness, the city's economy is enormous and diversified. According to the [U.S. Census Bureau](https://www.census.gov/), the Houston metropolitan area is home to more than 7.3 million people, making it the fifth-largest metro in the nation. Its flat, developable land and relatively permissive zoning allow the housing supply to expand in pace with demand, naturally moderating price spikes.

According to [the Austin Board of Realtors (ABoR)](https://www.abor.com/), average days on market in Austin have moderated from the frenzied 2021-2022 peak, but well-priced properties in desirable zip codes continue to attract multiple offers. Houston's [Houston Association of Realtors (HAR)](https://www.har.com/) data similarly shows a balanced-to-buyer-favorable market in many suburban corridors, giving purchasers more negotiating room.

Property tax rates are functionally equivalent: Austin-area effective rates average approximately 2.1%, while Houston-area properties average approximately 2.2%, according to the Texas Comptroller. Because assessed values are higher in Austin, the actual annual tax bill on a median-priced Austin home exceeds the Houston equivalent by several thousand dollars annually, a meaningful carrying cost difference for budget-conscious buyers.

## Flood Risk: A Critical Houston Consideration

One of the most consequential, and often underweighted, differences between Austin and Houston real estate is flood risk. [FEMA flood maps](https://www.fema.gov/flood-maps) designate significant portions of the Houston metropolitan area as Special Flood Hazard Areas (SFHAs). Hurricane Harvey in 2017 inundated more than 150,000 homes in the Houston metro, and subsequent storms have continued to cause repeated flooding in established neighborhoods.

Houston's geography is simply flood-prone: the region sits on flat coastal plain, has heavy clay soils with poor drainage, and receives an average of 50 inches of rainfall per year. While the Harris County Flood Control District has invested billions in infrastructure improvements since Harvey, buyers should always check FEMA flood zone designations for any specific Houston property and budget for flood insurance accordingly.

Austin is not flood-free, the Onion Creek basin and certain areas along Barton Creek have documented flood history, but the overall risk profile is considerably lower than Houston's. Most of Austin's desirable residential areas, including Westlake Hills, Tarrytown, and the Domain corridor, carry minimal flood exposure. Buyers in both cities should always review [FEMA's Flood Map Service Center](https://msc.fema.gov/portal/home) before purchasing.

The insurance cost differential is material. Houston homeowners in SFHA zones routinely pay $2,000 to $5,000+ annually for federally backed flood insurance, in addition to standard homeowner's coverage. Austin buyers in low-risk zones often forgo flood insurance entirely, meaningfully reducing their total cost of ownership.

## School Districts: Austin vs Houston's Best

School quality is one of the most consistent drivers of residential home values in any Texas market. According to the [Texas Education Agency (TEA)](https://tea.texas.gov/), Eanes ISD, which serves the Westlake Hills, Rollingwood, and portions of West Austin, is consistently ranked the top-performing independent school district in Texas. Westlake High School in Eanes ISD regularly earns national recognition for academic achievement, athletics, and college matriculation rates.

Austin ISD, which serves the city proper, has excellent schools in pockets, Crockett High School, McCallum High School, but varies significantly by zone. The Eanes ISD boundary commands a substantial price premium, and homes there rarely fall below $800,000.

In Houston, the best-ranked suburban districts are Katy ISD and Cy-Fair ISD, both located in the western and northwestern suburbs. These districts serve large, well-funded school systems and consistently earn TEA's highest ratings. Spring Branch ISD and Humble ISD also have strong reputations within their service zones.

For families who prioritize being in the absolute highest-ranked district in Texas, Eanes ISD is the clear choice, and living within its boundaries means paying a meaningful premium. For families comfortable with excellent-but-not-top-ranked districts, Houston's Katy ISD offers exceptional schooling at a significantly lower home price point.

## Investment Returns: Which City Has Performed Better?

For pure price appreciation, Austin has outperformed Houston in virtually every multi-year measurement window since 2000. [Redfin Research](https://www.redfin.com/news/data-center/) and [NAR](https://www.nar.realtor/research-and-statistics) data confirm that Austin metro home values have compounded at a higher rate than Houston metro values across 5-, 10-, and 20-year horizons.

However, cash-on-cash returns are a different story. Because Austin prices are significantly higher, the gross rental yield, annual rent divided by purchase price, is lower in Austin than in Houston. An investor purchasing a $320,000 Houston property with a gross rent of $2,200/month earns a higher initial yield than an investor paying $485,000 for an Austin property renting at $2,900/month. Houston's lower price-to-rent ratio makes it attractive for income-focused landlords.

Austin's short-term rental (STR) market adds another dimension. Austin's position as a premier live-music and festival city, home to SXSW, Austin City Limits, and Formula 1 at COTA, drives robust Airbnb and VRBO demand. STR operators in premium Austin zip codes have reported gross revenues of $60,000–$90,000+ annually for well-positioned properties. Houston's STR market is more limited due to zoning restrictions, lower tourist density, and competition from its vast hotel supply.

The verdict: Austin is the stronger appreciation play; Houston is a better starting point for cash flow-focused investors willing to trade appreciation upside for lower entry costs and higher initial yields.

## Lifestyle and Culture: Walkability vs Suburban Sprawl

Austin and Houston represent genuinely different lifestyle propositions. Austin's urban core, South Congress, Rainey Street, Second Street District, the Domain, is compact, walkable, and built around outdoor activity, live music, and farm-to-table dining. The Hill Country is 30 minutes west; Barton Springs is 10 minutes from downtown. The city attracts a young, educated, outdoor-enthusiast demographic that values density and connectivity.

Houston, by contrast, is a sprawling metropolis where car dependency is near-absolute. The city covers more than 670 square miles and lacks the natural geographic constraints that concentrate activity in Austin. The upside: Houston's diversity is extraordinary. The city is one of the most ethnically diverse in the United States, with unmatched culinary variety, a world-class museum district, major league sports across every major discipline, and the Texas Medical Center, the largest medical complex in the world.

For buyers who value walkability, outdoor recreation, and urban compactness, Austin wins clearly. For buyers who value cultural diversity, global cuisine, and access to major medical institutions, Houston presents compelling advantages, at a substantially lower price point.

Climate differences are worth noting: Houston's subtropical humidity and heat can be oppressive for newcomers from drier climates. Austin sits at a higher elevation with lower humidity, though summers are still hot by most standards. Both cities rarely see freezing temperatures in winter, though the February 2021 winter storm exposed infrastructure vulnerabilities across Texas that both metros are still addressing.

## Which Texas City Is Right for You?

The Austin vs Houston decision ultimately comes down to priorities. Choose Austin if you value: maximum long-term appreciation, access to the highest-ranked schools in Texas, lower flood risk, a walkable urban lifestyle, and proximity to the Hill Country. Expect to pay a premium of roughly $165,000 on the median-priced home and accept higher absolute property tax bills.

Choose Houston if you value: significantly lower entry prices, a globally diverse urban culture, the energy sector's massive employment base, access to the Texas Medical Center, and superior cash-flow potential on rental properties. Accept that flood risk requires due diligence on every property and that car-centric suburban living is the default lifestyle.

Many buyers who have lived in both cities report that Austin's scale, manageable, beautiful, and compact, is its greatest quality-of-life asset. As one of our clients who relocated from Houston's Energy Corridor summarized: "I expected to miss the space. I don't. I walk to dinner now."

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is Austin or Houston better for real estate investment? + :**

Austin has delivered superior price appreciation, approximately 41% over the past five years versus Houston's 28%. However, Houston offers lower entry prices (median ~$320K vs Austin's ~$485K), allowing investors to acquire more units for the same capital. Austin is better for appreciation-focused investors; Houston suits cash-flow-focused strategies given lower price-to-rent ratios.

**Q: Why are homes more expensive in Austin than Houston? + :**

Austin's higher prices reflect constrained land supply due to the Hill Country terrain to the west, a concentrated tech-industry job base (Apple, Tesla, Google, Meta), strong in-migration from high-cost states, and zoning restrictions that have historically limited density. Houston's flat coastal plain and more permissive land-use rules allow greater housing supply, which tempers price growth.

**Q: Does Austin or Houston have higher property taxes? + :**

The rates are nearly identical: Austin-area properties average approximately 2.1% effective rate while Houston-area properties average approximately 2.2%. Because assessed values are higher in Austin, the actual annual tax bill on a typical home is higher in Austin. Both cities lack a state income tax, making property taxes the primary local tax burden.

**Q: Which city has better schools, Austin or Houston? + :**

Austin's Eanes ISD is consistently ranked the #1 school district in Texas and among the top in the nation, serving the Westlake Hills and Rollingwood areas. Houston's strongest districts are Katy ISD and Cy-Fair ISD, which serve western and northwestern suburbs. For top-rated K-12 education, the Eanes ISD zone in Austin is generally considered the gold standard in Texas.

**Q: Is Austin or Houston better for families? + :**

Both cities serve families well but with different strengths. Austin offers lower flood risk, higher-ranked suburban school districts, and a more walkable urban core. Houston offers significantly more affordable housing, a globally diverse food and culture scene, and major medical facilities such as the Texas Medical Center. Families prioritizing school rankings and outdoor lifestyle typically prefer Austin; those prioritizing affordability and cultural diversity often choose Houston.
