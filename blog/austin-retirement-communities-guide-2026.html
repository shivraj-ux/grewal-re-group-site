Austin Retirement Communities & Neighborhoods Guide 2026

A comprehensive guide to retiring in Austin and Central Texas — from Sun City Georgetown’s 55+ amenities to Lake Travis waterfront living and the Texas Hill Country. No state income tax, world-class healthcare, and year-round sunshine.

By Shivraj Grewal · CLHMS Guild · CNE · TREC #736060 · May 8, 2026 · Austin, TX

The best Austin Texas retirement communities in 2026 include Sun City Georgetown for active adult 55+ living, Lago Vista and Marble Falls for lakefront retirement on the Highland Lakes, Hyde Park and South Congress for walkable urban retirement, and Dripping Springs and Wimberley in the Hill Country for a scenic, quieter pace. Texas offers no state income tax on Social Security or retirement income — a significant advantage for retirees relocating from California, New York, or other high-tax states.

Austin’s retirement market has experienced remarkable growth over the past decade, driven by a powerful convergence of factors: world-class medical infrastructure, a warm climate with over 300 sunny days per year, no Texas state income tax on retirement income, and a cultural richness that active retirees prize. The Austin metro — encompassing Travis, Williamson, Hays, Bastrop, and Caldwell counties — now offers retirement options spanning gated 55+ communities, lakefront estates, walkable urban neighborhoods, and quiet Hill Country retreats. This guide draws on data from the Social Security Administration, the Texas Comptroller, and real estate market data from the Austin Board of Realtors to help you choose the retirement community that best matches your lifestyle, health, and financial goals.

Why Retirees Are Choosing Austin and Central Texas

The migration of retirees to Austin and Central Texas has accelerated since 2020. Baby Boomers from California, New York, Illinois, and the Pacific Northwest have discovered that Texas offers something their home states cannot: a combination of financial relief and quality of life that is increasingly rare in the United States. Texas has no state income tax, which means that Social Security benefits, pension distributions, IRA withdrawals, and 401(k) income are not subject to state-level taxation. For a retiree receiving $60,000 per year in combined retirement income in California, eliminating state income tax alone can mean $3,000–$6,000 in annual savings — depending on filing status and deductions.

Beyond the tax advantage, Austin offers retirees access to two major hospital systems — Ascension Seton and St. David’s HealthCare — that together operate more than 25 facilities across the metro. Dell Seton Medical Center at The University of Texas is the region’s only Level I Trauma Center, and St. David’s Medical Center is nationally recognized for cardiac care. This level of medical infrastructure, combined with proximity to Austin’s live music, dining, and cultural institutions, creates a retirement environment that is genuinely difficult to match in the Sun Belt. [1]

Sun City Georgetown: Texas’s Premier Active Adult Community

Sun City Georgetown is the flagship 55+ active adult community in Texas — and one of the most successful Del Webb developments in the entire United States. Located approximately 30 miles north of downtown Austin in Georgetown, Sun City encompasses more than 9,000 homes spread across multiple phases of development that began in 1995 and continues today with new construction lots still available. The community sits within Williamson County, which has earned a national reputation for strong schools, low crime rates, and favorable business conditions — factors that also contribute to long-term real estate value.

The amenity package at Sun City Georgetown is genuinely exceptional by any standard. Residents have access to three championship golf courses, four fitness centers and indoor pools, outdoor pools, tennis and pickleball courts, softball fields, a dog park, and more than 150 resident-organized clubs spanning interests from woodworking and painting to hiking, cycling, and international travel. Home prices in Sun City range from approximately $350,000 for resale homes in established phases to $600,000+ for new construction and premium lots with Hill Country views. Monthly HOA fees of $150–$300 cover community amenities and exterior maintenance. The nearest major hospitals — Ascension Seton Williamson and St. David’s Georgetown Hospital — are 20–30 minutes away, providing reassuring access to acute care without the noise and density of urban living. Visit the community’s official site at suncity-texas.com or contact Georgetown, Texas for city services information. [2]

Lake Travis and Highland Lakes: Waterfront Retirement Living

For retirees drawn to the water, the Highland Lakes chain — a series of six reservoirs created by dams on the Colorado River — offers one of the most beautiful and practical retirement environments in Texas. Lake Travis, the largest and most developed of the Highland Lakes, anchors communities including Lakeway, Bee Cave, Hudson Bend, Lago Vista, and Point Venture. Marble Falls, at the upper end of the Highland Lakes chain, has emerged as a particularly desirable small-town retirement destination with a vibrant downtown, access to Lakes LBJ and Marble Falls, and a growing collection of restaurants and amenities that belie its modest size.

Waterfront and water-view homes on Lake Travis range from $450,000 for modest single-story homes with lake views to $900,000+ for custom lakefront properties with private boat docks. The Highland Lakes lifestyle centers on boating, kayaking, fishing, hiking, and the dramatic Texas Hill Country scenery that frames the lakes from every vantage point. Healthcare access is the primary consideration: Lakeway Regional Medical Center provides local emergency and specialty care, but major surgical centers and teaching hospitals remain 30–45 minutes away via State Highway 71 or 2222. For retirees in good health seeking a scenic, active lifestyle with strong community anchors, Lake Travis delivers an exceptional quality of life at prices well below comparable waterfront markets in Florida or the Carolina coast. [3]

Austin Proper: Urban Retirement for Active Seniors

Not every retiree wants to trade the city for a gated community or a lakefront retreat. For active seniors who have spent decades in urban environments and refuse to surrender walkability, dining, live music, and cultural institutions in retirement, Austin’s established inner-city neighborhoods offer a compelling alternative to the suburbs. Hyde Park — a historic neighborhood just north of UT Austin, characterized by Victorian and Craftsman homes and a walkable commercial corridor along Duval and 45th Streets — has long been popular with professors, artists, and professionals who value neighborhood character over amenities-package marketing. Hyde Park homes typically range from $650,000 to $1.2M+ for renovated single-family homes.

South Congress (SoCo) and the Travis Heights neighborhood offer a slightly warmer, more eclectic urban retirement environment, with proximity to Zilker Park, the Barton Springs Pool, and the South Congress Avenue dining corridor. Home prices in SoCo and Travis Heights range from $600,000 to $1.2M+, with both neighborhoods carrying some of Austin’s strongest walkability scores. The primary advantage for urban retirees is proximity to medical care: both Hyde Park and South Congress are within 10–15 minutes of Ascension Seton Medical Center, St. David’s Medical Center, and the Dell Medical School complex. Medicare.gov’s Care Compare lists multiple top-rated facilities within the Austin metro, making it one of the better-served urban retirement markets in the Sun Belt. [4]

Hill Country Retirement: Dripping Springs and Wimberley

The Texas Hill Country west and southwest of Austin has become one of the most sought-after retirement destinations in the state for retirees seeking peace, privacy, and natural beauty without sacrificing reasonable proximity to Austin’s amenities. Dripping Springs — a fast-growing city about 25 miles west of downtown Austin on US Highway 290 — has evolved from a rural pass-through into a genuine small-city community with excellent schools, a cluster of acclaimed wineries and distilleries along the 290 Wine Country corridor, and a growing retail and dining infrastructure that makes it increasingly self-sufficient. Home prices in Dripping Springs range from $400,000 for smaller homes on modest lots to $800,000+ for Hill Country estates with acreage and creek access.

Wimberley, about 40 miles southwest of Austin in Hays County, offers an even more intimate Hill Country retirement experience centered on the Cypress Creek and Blanco River corridors, the artisan shops and galleries of the Wimberley Square, and a community of creative professionals and active retirees who have consciously chosen a quieter pace. The lifestyle is defined by outdoor beauty — swimming in Blue Hole, hiking Jacobs Well Natural Area, and watching the fireflies in summer — rather than amenity packages and social programs. Both Dripping Springs and Wimberley are roughly 30 minutes from Buda and Kyle emergency facilities and 45–60 minutes from Austin’s major hospital campuses, which makes proximity-to-care an important consideration for retirees with more complex health needs. [5]

Texas Retirement Tax Benefits: Social Security, Pensions, and IRAs

Texas’s tax advantage for retirees is both substantial and straightforward. The state has no personal income tax — a position codified in the Texas Constitution and reinforced by the state’s political culture — which means that all forms of retirement income, including Social Security benefits, employer pension distributions, IRA and 401(k) withdrawals, and investment income, are not subject to state taxation. According to the Texas Comptroller’s office, there is simply no individual income tax return to file in Texas, regardless of income level.

The Social Security Administration notes that Social Security benefits may be subject to federal income tax depending on combined income thresholds, but Texas adds no additional state-level burden. Seniors who are 65 or older also qualify for an additional $10,000 homestead exemption from school taxes on top of the standard $40,000 exemption — and crucially, Texas law allows seniors to freeze their school district tax at the level assessed in the year they turn 65 (or the year they apply for the exemption), protecting them from future school tax increases even as property values rise. The AARP consistently ranks Texas among the most favorable states for retirees from a tax standpoint. [6]

Healthcare Infrastructure for Austin Area Retirees

The quality and accessibility of healthcare is the single most consequential factor for many retirement decisions, and Austin delivers on this front with a depth that surprises many relocating retirees. The two dominant health systems in the metro — Ascension Seton and St. David’s HealthCare — together operate more than 25 hospitals, outpatient surgery centers, imaging facilities, and specialty clinics across Travis, Williamson, Hays, and Bastrop counties. Dell Seton Medical Center at The University of Texas, a teaching hospital affiliated with the Dell Medical School, provides Austin with a Level I Trauma Center that handles the region’s most complex emergency cases.

For retirees on Medicare, the Austin area offers strong coverage across both major health systems. Medicare.gov rates St. David’s Medical Center highly for cardiac care, orthopedic procedures, and overall patient experience. Ascension Seton Williamson in Round Rock provides convenient access for Sun City Georgetown residents, while Ascension Seton Hays in Kyle serves the Dripping Springs and Wimberley corridors. The growing network of urgent care clinics, specialist offices, and outpatient facilities means that routine and specialty care is rarely more than 15–20 minutes away for most Austin-area retirees — a significant improvement over retirement markets in rural Texas or more remote Sun Belt destinations. [7]

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the best retirement communities near Austin Texas?

The top retirement communities near Austin in 2026 include Sun City Georgetown (Texas’s premier 55+ active adult community with over 9,000 homes and 150+ resident clubs), Lago Vista and Marble Falls for lakefront living on the Highland Lakes, Hyde Park and South Congress for urban walkable retirement within 10 minutes of major hospitals, and Dripping Springs and Wimberley in the Hill Country for a scenic, private lifestyle. Price ranges span from $350K at Sun City Georgetown to $1.2M+ for premium urban or waterfront properties.

Does Texas tax Social Security or retirement income?

No. Texas has no state income tax, which means Social Security benefits, pension income, 401(k) withdrawals, IRA distributions, and all other forms of retirement income are not subject to Texas state taxation. Retirees do not file a state income tax return in Texas. Additionally, homeowners age 65+ qualify for an additional $10,000 homestead exemption and can freeze their school district property tax at the level assessed the year they turn 65, protecting against future tax increases on their primary residence.

Is Austin a good place to retire in 2026?

Austin is an excellent place to retire for active seniors who value world-class medical facilities, a warm climate (300+ sunny days per year), no state income tax, live music and cultural amenities, and proximity to the Texas Hill Country and Highland Lakes. The Austin metro offers retirement options from gated 55+ communities in Georgetown to waterfront living on Lake Travis and urban neighborhoods near major hospitals. The main considerations are relatively high home prices and property taxes compared to smaller Texas markets.

What is Sun City Georgetown Texas?

Sun City Georgetown is a Del Webb 55+ active adult community in Georgetown, Texas, approximately 30 miles north of Austin. It is one of the largest active adult communities in the United States, with over 9,000 homes, three championship golf courses, four fitness centers, indoor and outdoor pools, tennis and pickleball courts, and more than 150 resident-organized clubs. Home prices range from approximately $350K for resale homes to $600K+ for new construction. Monthly HOA fees of $150–$300 cover community amenities and grounds maintenance.

What healthcare facilities are near Austin retirement communities?

Austin’s retirement market is served by two major hospital systems. Ascension Seton operates Dell Seton Medical Center (Level I Trauma Center), Seton Medical Center Austin, Ascension Seton Williamson in Round Rock (close to Sun City Georgetown), and Ascension Seton Hays in Kyle (serving the Hill Country corridor). St. David’s HealthCare operates six hospitals in the metro including St. David’s Medical Center (nationally ranked for cardiac care) and St. David’s Georgetown Hospital. Medicare advantage and supplement plans are widely accepted across both systems.
