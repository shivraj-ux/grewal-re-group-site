Austin Estate Sale Real Estate Guide 2026: Probate & More

By Shivraj Grewal · CLHMS Guild · CNE · TREC #736060 · Updated May 8, 2026
Estate Sales in Austin: Estate sales in Austin involve probate, administered through Travis County. Most use independent administration, allowing executors to sell without further court approval in 90 to 180 days. Heirs benefit from stepped-up basis, where inherited property's tax basis resets to its death value, often resulting in zero capital gains tax when sold soon after.

Texas Probate Basics: When Is It Required?

Probate is the legal process through which a deceased person's estate is formally administered, debts are paid, and assets are transferred to beneficiaries. In Texas, probate law is governed by the Texas Estates Code, which is administered through county-level courts. For Austin homeowners, that means Travis County Probate Court handles all filings related to properties located within the city and most of the surrounding area.

Probate is generally required when a person dies owning real property solely in their own name, without a valid mechanism to transfer ownership automatically. If the deceased had a properly drafted and funded revocable living trust, the property passes directly to the trust's beneficiaries and probate is avoided entirely. Similarly, homes titled with a right of survivorship, such as joint tenancy or community property with right of survivorship, transfer automatically to the surviving co-owner upon death. Texas also recognizes Transfer on Death (TOD) deeds, which allow property owners to name a beneficiary who inherits the property at death without court involvement.

When none of these planning tools are in place, the estate must go through probate before the property can be sold. This is not necessarily a problem, Texas has one of the most streamlined probate systems in the country, especially for estates where a valid will is in place, but it does add time, court costs, and procedural complexity to the sale process. The Travis County Probate Court processes hundreds of estate filings each year, and experienced local attorneys can often move through the process efficiently.

The Role of the Executor in Selling Austin Real Estate

The executor, named in the will, or appointed by the court when no will exists, is the legal representative of the estate. When it comes to real property, the executor's responsibilities are substantial: they must determine the property's fair market value, decide on the appropriate listing strategy, select a qualified listing agent, review and accept offers, and ultimately facilitate the closing. All of this must be done in compliance with Texas Estates Code and with the fiduciary duty to act in the estate's best interest.

Importantly, the executor is personally liable for decisions that breach their fiduciary duty. Selling a home significantly below market value, accepting an offer without adequate marketing exposure, or failing to disclose known material defects can all expose the executor to claims by the estate's beneficiaries. This is one reason why selecting an experienced, licensed Austin real estate agent, one with specific knowledge of estate transactions and Travis County requirements, is not just helpful but strategically important.

The Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) requires all agents representing estate sellers to maintain an active license and to follow the same disclosure and professional standards that apply to any other transaction. An estate sale is still a real estate transaction, and the standards are the same, even if the circumstances are more complex.

Independent vs. Dependent Administration in Texas

Texas offers two primary forms of estate administration, and the distinction has significant practical implications for the sale timeline.

Independent administration is the preferred and most common approach. Under this framework, authorized by the Texas Estates Code Section 101.001 et seq., the executor can manage and sell estate property without seeking court approval for each individual transaction. Once the court has admitted the will to probate and issued Letters Testamentary, typically within 4 to 8 weeks of filing, the executor has broad authority to act. This means the real estate listing can proceed largely like a standard sale: the executor signs the listing agreement, reviews offers, negotiates terms, and executes the closing documents.

Dependent administration applies when independent administration is not authorized by the will and the heirs do not unanimously agree to it, or when the court determines it is necessary to protect the estate's interests. Under dependent administration, the executor must obtain court approval before selling real property. This requires filing a motion, setting a hearing date, obtaining the court's order, and publishing notice of the sale in some cases. The process adds weeks or months to the transaction and increases costs, but it also provides additional protection to beneficiaries when the estate is contested or complex.

The State Bar of Texas offers a referral service for finding a qualified probate attorney in Travis County. Engaging legal counsel early in the process is one of the most important steps an executor can take.

Valuing the Estate Property: Appraisals and CMAs

Before any estate property can be listed for sale, it must be valued accurately. There are two primary tools for this: a formal appraisal from a licensed real estate appraiser, and a comparative market analysis (CMA) prepared by the listing agent.

A formal appraisal, conducted by an MAI or state-certified appraiser, establishes the fair market value at a specific date for legal, tax, and probate purposes. This "date-of-death appraisal" is critical for establishing the stepped-up cost basis (discussed below), and it may also be required by the probate court or requested by beneficiaries. Appraisers operating in the Austin market typically charge $400 to $800 for a residential appraisal, with costs varying by property size and complexity.

A CMA prepared by your listing agent provides current market context, what comparable homes are actually selling for in the current Austin market. According to Texas A&M Real Estate Center (TRERC), Austin-area median home prices have seen meaningful fluctuations since the 2022 peak. Understanding where the market stands at the time of listing is essential to pricing the estate property competitively, without leaving money on the table or sitting on the market unnecessarily.

Estate properties often require additional consideration: deferred maintenance, dated finishes, or contents that need to be cleared before listing. Your agent and a qualified stager or contractor can help assess whether pre-listing improvements are likely to generate a higher net return than selling as-is, a calculation that should always be analyzed in the context of the estate's carrying costs and timeline constraints.

Listing an Estate Property in Austin: What's Different

From a marketing standpoint, the goal of an estate listing is identical to any other sale: maximum qualified buyer exposure, competitive pricing, and professional presentation. But several practical differences require careful coordination.

First, the listing agreement must be signed by the executor, not the deceased owner or the beneficiaries. The listing agent should verify that Letters Testamentary have been issued before the agreement is executed. Without that court document, the executor lacks the legal authority to bind the estate.

Second, the seller's disclosure notice requirements under TREC rules apply to estate sales, though with important modifications. Executors who have never occupied the property must disclose that fact and are generally exempt from disclosing conditions they have no actual knowledge of, but they are still obligated to disclose known material defects. If the home has a history of foundation issues, roof leaks, or HVAC failures that the executor is aware of, those must be disclosed regardless of the estate context.

Third, estate properties often benefit from a "soft staging" approach, decluttering, cleaning, and removing personal belongings before professional photography. Many families choose to hold an estate liquidation sale for contents before the real estate listing goes live, which reduces chaos during showings and allows buyers to evaluate the home on its merits rather than through a lens of personal effects.

Austin's luxury market has significant depth in the $1M–$3M range, and estate properties in desirable neighborhoods, Tarrytown, Westlake Hills, Barton Creek, Rollingwood, can command strong prices even without full renovation. The key is accurate pricing and high-quality marketing through the MLS, print, digital, and brokerage network channels. Research from the Texas Comptroller's office consistently shows that properties sold through licensed agents with MLS exposure achieve higher prices than off-market or auction sales.

Capital Gains and Stepped-Up Basis When Selling an Inherited Home

One of the most significant financial advantages for heirs who inherit Austin real estate is the federal stepped-up cost basis rule. Under IRS Publication 551 and IRC Section 1014, the cost basis of inherited property is "stepped up" to the property's fair market value on the date of the original owner's death, not what the original owner paid for it.

Here is a concrete example: if an Austin homeowner purchased a home in 1998 for $180,000, and that home is worth $900,000 at the time of their death in 2026, the heir's cost basis is $900,000. If the heir sells the home shortly after inheriting it for $900,000, the taxable gain is zero, regardless of the original owner's substantial appreciation. Even if the home sells for $950,000, the heir's taxable gain is only $50,000, which may be eligible for the long-term capital gains rate (0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income) rather than ordinary income rates.

This stepped-up basis applies to property owned solely by the decedent. For community property, which is common in Texas marriages, both halves of the community property may receive a stepped-up basis upon the death of one spouse, under IRC Section 1014(b)(6). This is a uniquely favorable rule for Texas residents compared to those in common-law property states. Consulting with a CPA or estate attorney who understands both the Texas Estates Code and IRS rules is strongly recommended before any sale.

Texas has no state estate or inheritance tax. The federal estate tax applies only to estates exceeding $13.61 million for 2026, meaning the vast majority of Austin estates are not subject to federal estate tax at all. The Texas Comptroller confirms there is no state-level inheritance or estate tax applicable to Texas real property.

How to Work With an Estate Attorney and Real Estate Agent Together

Selling an Austin estate property efficiently requires close coordination between legal counsel, the real estate agent, a CPA, and sometimes a financial advisor. Each professional plays a distinct role, and the process runs most smoothly when everyone is communicating proactively from the beginning.

The estate attorney handles the probate filing, obtains Letters Testamentary, advises the executor on their fiduciary duties, and reviews the sale contract to ensure compliance with court requirements. In dependent administration, the attorney files the necessary motions and attends the confirmation hearing. The State Bar of Texas provides a lawyer referral service that can connect executors with qualified Travis County probate counsel.

The real estate agent handles the valuation, listing strategy, marketing, buyer negotiations, and coordination with the title company. An experienced Austin agent will know to align the closing timeline with probate court schedules, anticipate potential title issues related to the estate, and communicate transparently with all parties throughout.

The CPA or tax advisor ensures the estate's tax filings, including any final income tax return for the decedent, the estate's fiduciary income tax return (Form 1041), and potential federal estate tax return (Form 706), are prepared accurately. They also calculate the stepped-up basis and advise heirs on the tax consequences of the sale and any distributions.

Research from the Texas A&M Real Estate Center confirms that estate sellers who engage all three professionals, legal, real estate, and tax, consistently achieve better net outcomes than those who navigate the process without professional support. The complexity of Texas probate law, combined with Austin's competitive real estate market, makes the cost of professional guidance a well-justified investment.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does probate work when selling a home in Texas?

In Texas, probate is the court-supervised process of administering a deceased person's estate, paying debts, and distributing assets to heirs. When real property is involved, the executor named in the will (or appointed by the court) petitions Travis County Probate Court for Letters Testamentary, the legal document authorizing the executor to act on behalf of the estate. Under independent administration (the most common Texas form), the executor can then list and sell the property without further court approval. The process from filing to closing typically takes 90 to 180 days.

Do all Austin homes sold after death go through probate?

No. Several mechanisms allow Austin real estate to transfer outside probate entirely. Properties held in a revocable living trust pass directly to beneficiaries. Homes titled with right of survivorship (joint tenancy or community property with right of survivorship) transfer automatically to the surviving co-owner. Texas also recognizes Transfer on Death (TOD) deeds, which name a beneficiary who inherits at death without probate. If none of these structures are in place, probate is typically required before the property can be legally sold.

How long does it take to sell a home through probate in Travis County?

Under independent administration in Travis County, the full process, from date of death to closing, typically takes 90 to 180 days. The probate filing and executor appointment usually requires 4 to 8 weeks. Once Letters Testamentary are issued, the listing, marketing, offer, and closing can proceed much like a standard sale. Contested estates or dependent administration can extend the timeline to 6 to 18 months. Coordinating your real estate agent with your probate attorney from the outset helps minimize delays.

Can an executor sell a home without court approval in Texas?

Yes, in most cases. Under Texas Estates Code independent administration, which is available when authorized by the will or agreed to by all heirs, the executor can sell estate real property without obtaining a court order for each transaction. This makes Texas one of the most executor-friendly probate states in the country. Dependent administration, used less frequently, does require court confirmation of the sale.

Are there tax implications when inheriting and selling a home in Austin?

Yes, but they are often highly favorable. Heirs who inherit real property receive a stepped-up cost basis, the property's fair market value at the date of the owner's death, per IRS Publication 551. This means that if a home purchased in 1995 for $150,000 is worth $750,000 at inheritance, the heir's basis is $750,000. Selling for $750,000 generates zero taxable gain. Texas has no state estate or inheritance tax, and the federal estate tax applies only to estates exceeding $13.61 million in 2026. Most Austin heirs sell inherited property with little or no capital gains tax owed.
